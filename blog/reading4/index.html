Another Network Is Possible

It's unfortunate that indymedia lost it's traction in some areas, though I wonder what enables them to maintain active in other regions. It's interesting how there's a pattern of things becoming more local/less digitized in times of crisis. You'd think people would continue using it outside of times of crisis if it's shown to work well during.

Architecting Emergence: Why We Should Build A Virtual World Together In An Art Museum

I like the title and I think I agree with it. worldonawire.net is a very interesting website, but it seems like it hasn't been active in a while, since the last update/event was in 2021. Anyways, the experiment that was conducted has funny moments to it, but it always circles back to some kind of...bigger more serious things happening in the world. Like, it revolves around the same set of topics? Things to do with the economy, people's living situations, state of the world i guess.

The idea of Contagion, to me, sounds like a fancier way of explaining trends. They have a peak, and die down relatively quick, and a lot of people just become brain melded and go for this one thing. I don't know if there really is a connection. Convergence sounds more like the after effects of trends.

The Woman Who Taught Internet Strangers To Actually Care For One Another

From what I'm getting at, Stacy Horn's Echo platform had moderators that cared for the community in ways that we don't see being done today. It feels good knowing that women made up almost half of the population on echo, and how both sexes were involved in watching over conversations.

I think as technology and social media evolves, the norm for what a moderator does will change. Local online communities aren't as big of a thing because a mainstream social media platform just has everything in one place, making it super convenient. And in a mainstream media platform, it doesn't sound feasible for moderators to safeguard individual communities when they have so much intense things to flag.

The author makes an interesting point of how moderators went from keeping the communities happy to having to bear the burden of seeing a lot of nsfw things and making sure the users don't see them, and that they'll keep using the platform since it's "safe enough".

What I took away from this

If we're going to be online, we should make sure it's by the people and for the people. Online local communities may be worth having a comeback for
