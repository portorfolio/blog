The Web's Grain

It threw me off how Frank kinda dissed Apple's site. After I read his introduction about Hockney and his photograph pieces, I see what Frank is trying to say when he refers to the idea of web design being a building material, rather than a blank canvas. When you snap a photo, you already have something, but that something may not include everything you want it to, so you keep snapping more. That leads to more material, and the more you can do with it. Along with that, Hockney's pieces gradually become edgeless, and not so confined into a grid, which for sure ties into the fact that websites can feel literally endless. Edges can sometimes be a negative constraint.

When he showed example images of drawing then putting the box it literally blew. my. mind. Because he's right, I always start with a box. It's uncomfortable not to...but why should I let myself feel constrained like that? It makes me realize how the box acts as a safety net, and because of that it doesn't push my creativity, like, at all I fear. Also, that Diesel ad looks very cool and I love the colors.

This still leaves me kind of confused about the diss on very technically advanced looking websites. Is he saying how they lack intention, and are solely just for looks?

Heavy Lies the Digital Cloud

Okay first off this illustration is INSANE, shout out Victor I did indeed follow him right then and there. The weather analogy was incredibly interesting. It feels like common sense, but putting it into words helps you visualize just how grand it is, and it sets up the tone for the rest of the article. While reading about the "identities" that's spewed out from TikTok, it makes me wonder what percentage of me is actually me, and not just a shell of everything on the internet that was once trendy.

It's an intimidating thing to be aware of, how once something's out on the internet, it's out there. Whatever you search up is being stored, and can be accessed. The things you say are not just a one-and-done deal. And this data is being used, somehow, to benefit one, and harm the other. After reading this, I see why the author states that neutrality is not an appropriate term to use to describe the technology world.

On an unrelated note, I really love the fonts being used on this site.
